Teaching

Dynamic pedagogy, research integration, and inclusive mentorship

University of Alberta Graduate Students Teaching Award, 2020

Recognized for dynamic, embodied pedagogical methods that transform how students engage with cognitive neuroscience.

Teaching Philosophy

I believe cognitive neuroscience becomes most accessible when students can visualize and experience concepts directly. Teaching Brain and Behavior to classes of nearly 400 students, I transform lecture halls into neural circuits—using doorways as synaptic channels and the podium space to demonstrate neurotransmitter release. When explaining bipolar disorder, I create a mood continuum across the classroom, physically demonstrating the inability to "apply brakes" between extremes.

This embodied approach, combined with spontaneous connections to students' lived experiences, helps anchor abstract concepts in memorable frameworks. I've implemented large-scale in-class experiments using portable EEG devices, transforming students from passive consumers of knowledge into active contributors to the field.

Connecting with Diverse Students

My teaching spans three distinct academic environments: the multicultural classrooms of the City College of New York, the research-intensive University of Alberta, and Rutgers University in New Jersey. Each context has deepened my commitment to fostering inclusive learning communities where all students feel valued and supported.

As a multilingual educator fluent in English, Spanish, and Portuguese, I bring cross-cultural insights into the classroom. My international research experience—including EEG fieldwork in rural Malawi where I trained local volunteers—informs how I help students understand that neuroscience research can serve diverse populations while examining potential biases in traditional approaches.

I establish clear expectations while providing comprehensive, fair assessment and personalized feedback. My commitment to undergraduate mentorship extends beyond the classroom: I have co-authored publications with undergraduate researchers and provided career guidance that has helped students successfully enter graduate and medical programs.

Courses I Can Teach

My expertise in EEG/ERP methodologies, human population research, and experimental design prepares me to teach a range of courses:

  • Introductory Behavioral Neuroscience or Cognitive Neuroscience
  • Mobile Neuroscience: Studying the Brain in Action
  • Addiction Neuroscience: From Mechanisms to Treatment
  • Research Methods and Statistics
  • Laboratory courses integrating EEG techniques

My research program studying attention, cognitive control, and addiction provides rich opportunities for undergraduate thesis projects, summer research experiences, and potential co-authored publications.
