About

I am a cognitive neuroscientist studying how reward processing dysfunction contributes to substance use disorders—and how we can fix it. My research combines transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS), EEG, and diffusion MRI to develop targeted interventions that restore reward circuitry function in individuals with nicotine dependence.

Beyond the lab, I'm committed to taking neuroscience into the real world. During my Ph.D. at the University of Alberta, I developed mobile EEG methods to study attention during skateboarding and urban cycling—including the first successful EEG recordings during active skateboarding. I've also conducted EEG fieldwork in rural Malawi as part of an international child development study, training local volunteers in electrophysiological data collection.

I teach courses in brain and behavior, cognitive psychology, and neuropsychology at Rutgers University and the City College of New York. My teaching emphasizes embodied, experiential learning—transforming lecture halls into neural circuits and bringing portable EEG into the classroom. I received the University of Alberta Graduate Students Teaching Award in 2020 for this dynamic pedagogical approach.
